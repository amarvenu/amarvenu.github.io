Executive Summary

  • Among the 7,720 SFPC cases with at least one normalized stage event, the modal lifecycle is a single-meeting approval: 3,427 cases (44.4%).
  • Unresolved Commission paths are not rare: 1,019 cases (13.2%) end in one or more continuances without an observed later decision in the normalized SFPC record.
  • Environmental cases are the slowest major family in the current SFPC data, with a median observed Commission span of 56 days.
  • Participation intensity tracks delay in the descriptive data: cases with 26+ unique speakers average 1.68 continuances and a median Commission span of 91 days.
  • The repo is already doing nontrivial staff-report extraction, not “hardly any”: about 66.8% of unique textable staff reports are currently extracted, yielding 7,729 condition rows, 624 DR attribute rows, and 7,760 land-use rows.
  • But the highest-value lifecycle fields are still mostly missing from extraction: continuance reasons, project event histories, statutory basis, coalition/issue tagging in comments, and the richer numeric snapshot codebook. That is why only 108 cases currently support a reasonably usable first-versus-last unit-count comparison.
What the current LLM extraction is really doing
It is not accurate to say the repo is doing “hardly any” staff-report extraction. The current pipeline has already extracted about 66.8% of unique textable staff reports (2,577 of 3,860), producing 7,729 condition rows, 624 DR-attribute rows, and 7,760 land-use rows. The problem is different: the extraction we have is broad enough to summarize reports, but still too shallow on the exact lifecycle features that matter most for the research agenda.

LLM extraction gap in this section
The current lifecycle tables know that a case was continued, approved, denied, or decided as a DR matter. They mostly do not know why a continuance happened, what off-docket discussion preceded it, whether the sponsor was asked for revisions, or how the legal framing changed between hearings. Those are exactly the kinds of event-level fields an additional staff-report / minutes extraction pass would add.

Within the SFPC record itself, the dominant unresolved pathway is not denial but aging continuance. Many unresolved cases were last seen as continuances or hearings years before the current data endpoint, suggesting that they likely went inactive, withdrew, or otherwise left the observable Commission record without a clean terminal vote.

6. Where additional staff-report LLM extraction would most improve the report

  1. Continuance reasons and project events. The current lifecycle tables tell us that a case continued, but usually not why. Extracting dated events and continuance reasons from staff reports and related materials would turn lifecycle counts into a real narrative timeline.
  2. Richer numeric snapshots. Running the snapshot codebook batch would materially improve measurement of unit-count changes, height changes, affordability changes, and the use of waivers/concessions across hearings.
  3. Statutory-basis extraction. Cases like 2588 Mission turn on density bonus, HAA, CEQA tiering, and similar legal mechanisms. Those are only weakly visible today. A targeted extraction for these fields would let the report say which legal pathways are associated with which lifecycle shapes.
  4. Comment and coalition content. The current report can count speakers, but not yet say which organizations repeatedly appear, what they argue, or whether participation is mostly about affordability, design, tenant protections, environmental concerns, or something else.
  5. Condition-theme precision. The existing condition extraction is already useful, but a substantial share of substantive conditions still fall into other or null theme buckets. Refining that taxonomy would help distinguish “design/aesthetic” concessions from “public interest” conditions much more cleanly.
  6. Decision-document linkage. The standalone motion/resolution corpus is still weakly linked back to cases. Improving that join would make it easier to recover formal findings language and adopted conditions for final-decision analysis.
Bottom line on extraction
The repo is already doing a fair amount of LLM extraction. The real issue is that the current extraction is strongest on report-level summaries and weaker on lifecycle-level structure. The next investment should not just be “more extraction,” but extraction aimed specifically at events, reasons, legal pathways, and per-hearing design snapshots.

Methods and caveats

  • The lifecycle sample in this report is 7,720 case bases with at least one normalized SFPC stage event, not the full 10,430 rows in the broader planning_cases table.
  • Lifecycle patterns are measured at the meeting-date level: if a case has multiple raw stage rows on one meeting date, the report collapses them to a single milestone label for sequence analysis.
  • Recommendation-alignment analysis uses the existing decisions.recommendation_alignment field and linked public-comment counts at the agenda-item level.
  • Condition analysis uses the current LLM-extracted staff_report_conditions table. The taxonomy is already useful, but theme precision is incomplete.
  • Project-change analysis uses residential-unit counts rolled up from staff_report_land_use. This is the noisiest part of the current report and should be treated as provisional pending richer snapshot extraction.
  • This report is intentionally SFPC-only. It does not estimate post-entitlement attrition, building-permit progression, certificates of occupancy, or the dollar cost of delay, because those require APR or external cost data.
